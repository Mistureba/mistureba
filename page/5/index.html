2016 07 19 narrative

I think I’ve got some good starting points here, but I’m also gonna start by dividing what I mean by “characterization” into a couple groups because I think we’re looking at the word differently, SO

theres two steps to characterization where things can go wrong:

  • the storyteller figuring out the ins and outs of the character, who they are, character traits, backstory, etc

  • how the audience figures these things out (note: this is still on the part of the author! im not talking about the audience misunderstanding characterization; different interpretations of things are allowed even if they are bad but the way the storyteller conveys the character is still very important

the first step is where most people responded, so i’ll start there, too; it’s mostly the second part i have in mind, though.

@theartisticanon this is a good observation, but it’s not quite what i wanna talk about; my fault for not being more clear, sorry! bad characterization on the part of the audience would be something else to talk about another time!

@arw5 @curtaincomedy I feel like stereotypes can sometimes be a good place to start with creating a character, but there is a problem when that base level is all you use. the whole deal with “flawed” vs “perfect”, too, though, i think may largely be a problem because writers have taken it into their heads that these are important terms in the first place - imo, you can have a character who is either extremely flawed or seems to have very few flaws who is still a good character, because to my mind the mark of a good character is that they feel like a real person. elements can be exaggerated far beyond how any actual real person might behave, sure, but that’s alright as long as they still feel real.

An example of the “too perfect” stereotype i think most often shows up in hollywood movies would be, for example, the “girl who is almost perfect in every way but is just a bit clumsy.” you see this a lot in some anime, in typical YA novels, and in bad romcoms - and the thing is even this sort of character type can work, but that depends on the execution of the character more than anything else. however, making a character like this a main character might not be a good idea, not unless you’ve got plans to make the character more complex - its a good minor character, potentially.

and as i was typing this @dimespin used the same example, and went exactly where I was gonna go with it, lol

because, scooting over to execution - and this, mind you, is the part where the audience learns about the character, the thing about “girl who is mostly perfect but is also clumsy” is that she has to be clumsy for the entirety of the story, or at least until you get to a point where it’s going to actually be addressed. you gotta constantly have the character exiting stage left to the sound of things crashing, have plenty of oops moments - mind you when i say constantly i dont mean ‘every thirty seconds,’ just have it actually be a regular thing. and there’s things you can do with that! how does the character feel about her own clumsiness? does it mess with her self esteem a lot? does she think less of herself because of how hard it is to walk in heels? or, as a minor character, she could be occasional comedic relief! heck, you could combine the two if you wanted. but you can’t just throw a dart at a board of character flaws and slap ‘clumsy’ on to make her feel human. Or, maybe she’s in denial about being clumsy! Maybe her clumsiness is a disguise and she’s actually and android still learning how to walk!! Maybe—

Okay, here’s the point i’m getting at - if you want your characters to feel real, you can’t just slap flaws on them, you have to remember that the character thinks, too. what’s up with these flaws? what does the character think of their own flaws? are they aware of them or in denial? you don’t even have to actively address all these things, even; just keep them in mind, because they will affect the way your character acts. And you also need to keep the character consistent; don’t just slip in character traits whenever you remember them.

and now, the BIG thing about execution in terms of characterization - everything your character does is part of it. Everything they say, how they say it, what choices they make, how they do everything from big momentous things to tiny everyday things. All of it conveys aspects of your character, and the masters of characterization will, within a single scene make you feel like you know a character (mind you, that doesn’t mean your initial perception of the character will be the same through the whole story; just that they very quickly feel like someone who could really exist.)

A good example of this in action is in this video - an animation test from Big Hero Six, which doubles as a character study. It’s a super simple scene; no dialogue, just each character doing the same thing - walking into a room and sitting down in a chair. But the way they do those things speaks VOLUMES about each of them, and you feel like each is someone you might actually see in a little corner coffee shop. Fred, for example, is a favorite of mine from this reel, so I’ll spotlight him a bit. Bad characterization for Fred would just be saying “he really likes comics.” Instead, what we get in his segment is, yes, he really likes comics, but we can also tell a lot more about him - he’s REALLY passionate about the things he likes, he gets intensely focused on those things (notice how he gets hit with the door and barely even notices?), he’s very physically expressive and loud and doesn’t particularly care what anyone thinks about him, and he’s probably not the brightest guy around but he’s certainly extroverted and you get the impression that he’s probably pretty friendly, too. he’s certainly very comfortable in public, thats for sure!

all that from a clip of him walking in through a door and sitting at a table. And each of the segments conveys lots of info like that, too; i think it’s a great character exercise - how much of your character can you convey through a super simple scene like this?

other tricks to help with characterization - remember, every character is going to have something that pushes them too far, no matter how patient! what ‘too far’ looks like, what it is, how they respond to being pushed over their limit - that’s all stuff that will say a lot about your character. also, unless a lack of expression is a character trait itself (which can still be a lot of fun - look at Raven from the original animated Teen Titans show), let your character make expressions and show emotion! how expressive they are will vary a lot, of course, but even so; whether they react to a situation with a raised eyebrow or by leaping onto the nearest set of shoulders, they’re gonna react. in fact, overall, how your character reacts to situations is gonna be a lot more important than just words you think up to summarize the character in a nutshell. And finally, and possibly most importantly, every character has a set of motivations; maybe it’s one thing, maybe its a lot of things, but keeping those in mind is gonna really help you out. Even if that motivation is something super mundane and unrelated to the main plot (like a character from a story about some Epic Grand Adventure whose only goal is to graduate high school), they still should be kept in mind!

some other people said stuff too and i might talk about those in other posts, and if theres anything ppl wanna add or ask im up for it! if u read this far you are wonderful ty
